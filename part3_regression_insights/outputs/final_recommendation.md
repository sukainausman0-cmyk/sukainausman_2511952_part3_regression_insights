# Final Recommendation

The strongest factors associated with monthly sales are footfall, marketing spend, inventory availability, customer rating, and store/region differences. The selected multiple regression model explains a high share of sales variation with R-squared of 0.9862, so it is the best model for business decision-making among the models tested.

Leadership should focus first on increasing qualified footfall, protecting inventory availability, and improving customer experience. Marketing spend appears useful, but it should be allocated toward stores and campaigns where it can create measurable traffic and conversion. Inventory availability is important because stores cannot convert demand when products are unavailable.

Average discount should not be over-interpreted as a simple lever to increase sales. In this regression it has a negative relationship, which may mean discounts are being used when stores already face weak demand or lower-value baskets. Region and store type dummy variables are useful for planning, but they should be interpreted as location and format signals rather than proof that one region automatically causes higher sales.

Recommended action: prioritize marketing and inventory support for stores with strong footfall potential, investigate underperforming stores with large negative residuals, and study high positive residual stores to identify transferable practices.

Regression shows association, not automatic causation. Before making large spending changes, leadership should validate the findings with experiments, A/B tests, or before-and-after campaign analysis.
